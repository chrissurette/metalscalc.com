Quick math

Melt value = weight in troy ounces × purity × live spot price. The calculators below do it for you.

About this hub

Coins have two kinds of value: melt value (the metal content) and numismatic value (what collectors will pay). For common-date and worn coins, melt value usually rules — and that is exactly what these calculators estimate from weight, purity, and the live spot price.

Pre-1965 U.S. dimes, quarters, and half dollars are 90% silver. Many bullion coins are .999 fine silver or pure gold. Weigh the coins, pick the matching purity, and total the lot below.

FAQ

Coins questions

How do I calculate the melt value of a coin?

Find the coin's metal weight and purity, convert to troy ounces, and multiply by the live spot price. For 90% silver coins, weighing the lot and using the .900 setting is fastest.

How much silver is in a pre-1965 quarter?

A 90% silver quarter contains about 0.1808 troy ounces of pure silver. A dime has about 0.0723 ozt and a half dollar about 0.3617 ozt.

What is the difference between melt value and numismatic value?

Melt value is the metal content. Numismatic value is the collector premium for rarity, condition, or date. Rare coins can be worth far more than melt — have those appraised.
