War Nickel

War Nickel Value (1942–1945)

How much is a war nickel worth? Melt value of 35% silver Jefferson war nickels (1942–1945) across spot prices, plus how to identify them.

Calculate coin value

During World War II, the U.S. removed nickel from the five-cent coin for the war effort and made "war nickels" from a 35% silver alloy. Minted from mid-1942 through 1945, each war nickel contains about 0.0563 troy ounces of silver — the only circulating U.S. nickels that contain silver.

You can identify a war nickel by the large mint mark (P, D, or S) above Monticello on the reverse. The chart below shows their melt value across silver spot prices.

FAQ

War Nickel questions

Which nickels are silver?

Only the wartime "war nickels" minted from mid-1942 through 1945 contain silver (35%). All other U.S. nickels are copper-nickel with no silver.

How do I identify a war nickel?

Look for a large mint mark — P, D, or S — above the dome of Monticello on the reverse. That large mint mark indicates the 35% silver wartime composition.

How much silver is in a war nickel?

A war nickel contains about 0.0563 troy ounces of pure silver. Its melt value is 0.0563 × the silver spot price.

How much is a war nickel worth?

At a $58 silver spot, a war nickel holds about $3.26 in melt value. Better dates and grades can carry a collector premium.
